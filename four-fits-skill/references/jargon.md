# Four Fits — Jargon Buster

The framework borrows vocabulary from venture, product, marketing and finance. A founder
shouldn't need a separate degree to use the diagnostic. When a term comes up that the founder
might not know, define it inline in plain English rather than sending them away — pull the
definition from here.

## Money and economics

- **ARPA / ARPU** (Average Revenue Per Account / Per User) — the average annual revenue from one
  customer. 100 customers paying $10k/year each = $10k ARPA.
- **ACV** (Annual Contract Value) — the total annual value of a customer's contract, especially
  for enterprise deals.
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost) — what it costs, on average, to win one paying customer:
  ad spend, sales team time, content costs, all in.
- **LTV** (Lifetime Value) — the total revenue you expect from one customer over their whole life
  with you. A $10k/year customer who stays 3 years has a $30k LTV.
- **LTV / CAC** — a ratio; healthy target is 3× or higher. Below 1× you lose money on every sale.
- **Payback period** — how many months of revenue from a new customer it takes to recover what
  you spent acquiring them. Healthy SaaS: under 12–18 months.
- **ARR / MRR** (Annual / Monthly Recurring Revenue) — the predictable revenue you'd earn over a
  year (or month) if nothing changed today. The headline number for subscription businesses.
- **Unit economics** — the maths of a single customer: revenue earned, cost to acquire, cost to
  serve, profit. If the unit economics don't work, scaling makes things worse, not better.

## Channels and sales motion

- **Channel** — the route by which a customer first discovers the product.
- **Power Law of Distribution** — the empirical pattern that early-stage companies get 70%+ of
  acquisition from one channel, not several.
- **Self-serve** — customers find, sign up, and start using the product with no salesperson.
- **PLG** (Product-Led Growth) — self-serve plus a deliberate strategy of using the product
  itself as the acquisition and conversion engine, often via free/freemium tiers and viral loops.
- **Inside sales** — a sales team that closes over phone, email and video; suits mid-sized deals.
- **Field / enterprise sales** — reps who build long relationships and close large deals over
  months; high CAC.
- **Inbound / Outbound** — inbound: prospects find you (SEO, content, referrals). Outbound: you
  reach prospects who haven't heard of you (cold email, cold calls).
- **SEO** — ranking on the Google searches your buyers type; earns free traffic once it works.
- **CAC–ARPU spectrum / danger zone** — viable business models sit at the extremes (low cost +
  low revenue per customer, or high + high). The deadly middle is high cost to acquire paired
  with low revenue per customer.

## Product and user experience

- **Aha moment** — the single, specific, observable moment a user *feels* what the product does
  for them. Not "they understood it" — they experienced it.
- **Time to Value (TTV)** — how long from starting (sign-up, contract) to reaching the Aha moment.
- **Hook** — the thing that pulls the user in and, ideally, forms a habit.
- **Stickiness** — why users come back: habit, network effect, accumulated data, switching cost.
- **Onboarding** — the journey from first sign-up to Aha. Treated here as a separate product whose
  only job is to get the user to Aha fast.
- **Retention / Churn** — retention: the share of users who keep coming back over time (the best
  signal of product-market fit). Churn: its opposite — the rate customers leave.
- **Must-have moment / the Sean Ellis test** — ask users how disappointed they'd be if the product
  vanished; 40%+ saying "very disappointed" indicates product-market fit.
- **Value Proposition** — the actual value delivered, stated simply. The promise on the homepage.

## Market and customers

- **ICP** (Ideal Customer Profile) — the exact kind of customer the product is built for; the
  closer to a real, specific human, the better.
- **Persona** — a profile of a typical user. The framework wants one *individual*, not a vague
  marketing composite.
- **PMF** (Product-Market Fit) — the state where a real market wants what you're building enough
  to pay and tell others.
- **GTM** (Go-to-Market) — the overall plan for how the product reaches the customer; combines
  Channel, Model and sales motion.
- **TAM / SAM / SOM** — concentric circles of market size: everyone who could buy → everyone you
  could reach → the slice you'll realistically win.
- **The Janz animals** — Christoph Janz's shorthand for customer segments by annual revenue per
  customer: Mouse ($100), Rabbit ($1k), Deer ($10k), Elephant ($100k), Whale ($1M). Each carries
  different implications for virality, sales motion and the number of customers needed.
- **Laid, Made, or Paid** — shorthand for the three core human motivations behind a purchase:
  status/belonging/attraction (Laid), achievement/mastery/advancement (Made), and
  money/security/savings (Paid).

## Business model patterns (Strategyzer)

- **Subscription** — customers pay regularly for ongoing access. Standard SaaS.
- **Freemium** — a free tier that converts a fraction of users to paid.
- **Transactional** — pay per use or per purchase, no recurring relationship.
- **Marketplace** — you connect two sides of a transaction and take a cut.
- **Multi-sided platform** — three or more user types interact, with different value flows.
- **Razor-and-blade** — sell the durable thing cheap, make money on the consumable (Gillette,
  printer ink).
- **Pay-per-use** — the customer pays in proportion to consumption (cloud compute).
- **Aggregator** — you become the default place to find supply, taking a cut as gatekeeper.
- **Open-source-with-paid-services** — core product free and open; revenue from support, hosting,
  premium features.
- **Long tail** — serve a vast number of niche customers with small individual revenue.
- **Who-pays inversion** — the user isn't the buyer (free for users, advertisers pay; or free for
  individuals, employer pays).
- **Third-party subsidised** — someone other than the user picks up the bill, because the product
  creates value for that third party (Apple getting telcos to subsidise iPhones).

## Framework-specific terms

- **Fit** — the joint between two adjacent elements. It either matches or it doesn't yet.
- **Connection (C1–C13)** — a specific named link between two boxes that the diagnostic walks.
- **Cascade** — a cross-fit failure where one upstream gap creates pressure on a fit two steps
  downstream. The most important pattern the diagnostic looks for.
- **Aligned / Partial / Needs work** — the three states the diagnostic uses for each fit and
  connection. Aligned = the two sides match. Partial = some alignment, a meaningful gap. Needs
  work = a significant mismatch.
- **Smooth Sailer / Tugboat** (Balfour) — companies where growth feels easy versus hard. Tugboats
  often have product-market fit but are missing one of the other three fits.
- **Macro problem / Micro problem** — the systemic "why it matters" versus the specific,
  addressable bottleneck inside it. You solve the macro by picking the right micro.
