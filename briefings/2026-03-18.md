### 1. US Treasury Publishes Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework
**Gap:** Regulation | **Type:** Reinforces
**Source:** [US Treasury AI risk framework for financial firms — ResultSense](https://www.resultsense.com/news/2026-03-17-us-treasury-publishes-ai-risk-framework-for-financial-services)

The US Department of the Treasury released the Financial Services AI Risk Management Framework (FS AI RMF), a sector-specific extension of the NIST framework defining 230 control objectives across four governance functions: govern, map, measure, and manage. Published alongside a shared AI Lexicon with common definitions for 230+ AI concepts, the framework was developed via a public-private partnership between Treasury's FBIIC and the Financial Services Sector Coordinating Council. This is the clearest US regulatory signal yet that agentic AI deployments inside banks will need formal governance architecture — precisely the layer you are building.

### 8. EU AI Act High-Risk Rules for Financial AI Taking Effect August 2026
**Gap:** Regulation | **Type:** Reinforces
**Source:** [AI Regulation in Financial Services: Turning Principles into Practice — BCLP](https://www.bclplaw.com/en-US/events-insights-news/ai-regulation-in-financial-services-turning-principles-into-practice.html)

By August 2, 2026, EU-facing financial institutions must comply with transparency requirements and safeguards for high-risk AI systems — specifically including creditworthiness assessment and credit scoring. Legal analysis from BCLP confirms no direct contradictions between the AI Act and existing EU banking regulation, but the co-existence of multiple supervising authorities creates significant compliance overhead. This is a hard deadline that makes the "regulated infrastructure layer for agentic AI" conversation urgent rather than theoretical for any institution with EU exposure.
