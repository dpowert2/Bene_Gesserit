# Agentic AI in Financial Services — Weekly Intelligence Report
**Date:** 13 March 2026
**Coverage:** Inaugural report — week ending 13 March 2026

---

## Overview

This inaugural report covers the first full week of systematic scouting for early-stage companies building the infrastructure and applications layer for agentic AI in financial services. Twenty-one companies have been surfaced and evaluated across six strategic gap categories. The overall signal is strong: the market is moving quickly, institutional investors and incumbents are committing capital at pace, and regulatory frameworks are beginning to formalise AI agent obligations — all of which accelerate the strategic rationale for establishing a regulated infrastructure position now.

---

## Gap Coverage

### Personal Agent
The personal agent category — startups building agentic finance assistants for consumers and advisors — has two notable entries this week. **Mine (MoneyGPT)**, which raised a $14M Series A, is building an AI financial advisor that covers discovery, planning, and portfolio execution. It represents the "front door" agent archetype the thesis anticipates connecting to regulated infrastructure. **Tendi** offers a different angle: rather than competing on AI capability, it focuses on institutional connectivity — plugging into 12,000+ financial institutions globally — which makes it a potential data and execution layer for more sophisticated agents built on top.

The category has fewer entrants than others, and the consumer-facing nature of both companies means finserv regulatory readiness is the key variable to evaluate in deeper diligence.

### Compliance
The compliance category is the most funded and fastest-moving of the six gaps. **Bretton AI** (formerly Greenlite AI) has closed a $75M Series B, counting Robinhood, Mercury, and Gusto among its customers and having completed 1.2M+ investigations. It is the dominant scale player in AI-driven financial crime compliance. **Unit21** is an established fraud and AML risk operations platform, recently ranked in the RegTech100 2026 list.

The most architecturally interesting new entrant is **Sphinx**, which raised $7.1M from Cherry Ventures and Y Combinator. Sphinx does not replace existing compliance systems — it deploys browser-native AI agents that operate *inside* the tools institutions already use (case management platforms, portals, email, PDFs), achieving 94% false positive reduction at Equals Money without requiring any system integration. This non-disruptive architecture directly addresses the adoption barrier that has slowed compliance AI adoption in regulated institutions.

The directional preference within this gap is leaning toward orchestration-layer compliance rather than individual workflow automation — companies that can coordinate and govern agent compliance behaviour across multiple systems, rather than automating a single investigation type.

### Tax Optimisation
Three companies are in scope across the tax gap: **TaxDown** (€4M from BBVA Spark), **Mezzi**, and **Hive Tax AI**. Early feedback signals a clear preference distinction: tax *filing* tools are not of strategic interest, but portfolio-integrated tax intelligence — automated tax-loss harvesting, capital gains planning, and tax-aware portfolio rebalancing that operates as an ongoing agent rather than a seasonal tool — is highly relevant.

TaxDown is primarily a tax filing platform; Mezzi and Hive Tax AI are closer to the portfolio optimisation model. None of the three is an exact fit with the ideal profile. The ideal company in this gap — one that operates as a continuous tax intelligence agent, tightly integrated with portfolio management systems, and capable of surfacing tax benefits as positions change in real time — has not yet been surfaced. This should be a priority for the next scan cycle.

### Agent Orchestration
Five companies are in scope for the orchestration gap. The standout new entrant is **Lyzr**, which closed a $14.5M Series A+ led by Accenture Ventures and is being deployed in Accenture's banking and insurance client engagements. Its on-premises architecture — keeping agent data within institutional boundaries — is the explicit reason regulated enterprises are selecting it over cloud-hosted alternatives. The Accenture relationship is significant: it means Lyzr is not selling to banks, it is being sold *into* banks by one of the most trusted technology integrators in financial services.

The broader orchestration market received a significant validation signal from **Rogo's $75M Series C**, co-invested by J.P. Morgan and Wells Fargo — the first known case of two major Wall Street banks co-investing in an orchestration infrastructure provider. This pattern suggests the window for establishing orchestration partnerships with major financial institutions is open now and may begin to close as preferred vendors are selected.

**Salesforce Agentforce** hitting approximately $1.8B in ARR with 22,000 enterprise deals is the most significant external challenge signal of the week. It demonstrates that large incumbents with existing enterprise relationships can scale agentic AI into major revenue lines within a single fiscal year — which compresses the timeline for positioning regulated infrastructure before front-office orchestration layers lock in.

### AI Safety & Guardrails
The guardrails category received major validation this week. **Kai** emerged from stealth with $125M, founded by the creators of Claroty and SecurityMatters, and has already generated seven-figure bookings across Fortune 500 customers in its first ten months. Financial services infrastructure is among its target verticals.

The most thesis-aligned company in the pipeline is **Overmind** — a London-based startup building the supervision and monitoring layer for AI agents in regulated industries, founded by a former MI5, MI6, and GCHQ AI systems builder. With a €2.3M seed round, European geography, and an explicit focus on fintech, legal, and healthcare, Overmind maps precisely onto the regulated deployment context. The security intelligence pedigree of the founding team adds a dimension of credibility for institutions managing both operational and adversarial agent risks.

The regulatory environment is now hardening around this category: FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report has for the first time formally categorised AI agents as a distinct compliance category, signalling that agent-specific compliance obligations will be formalised for broker-dealers and registered investment advisers within the next 12–18 months. The EU AI Act deadline of August 2026 adds a parallel compliance forcing function for European institutions. These regulatory developments are converting guardrails from a product preference into a procurement obligation.

### Agent Authentication & Security
This category has the clearest early directional signal. **t54 Labs** (Seed, $5M) is building agent identity standards and verification infrastructure for the crypto and fintech ecosystem. **Oasis Security** ($75M total raised, Series A extension) manages non-human identity at enterprise scale — purpose-built for the challenge of thousands of AI agents operating across an organisation's systems. **Sapiom** ($15.75M Seed, backed by Anthropic, Okta Ventures, Accenture, and Coinbase Ventures) is building the financial identity and micro-payment rails that allow AI agents to autonomously acquire tools and services. **Alter** is building zero-trust authentication and authorisation wrapping every tool call an agent makes.

The thesis statement is most directly expressed by this category: if you is to be the infrastructure layer that agents connect to, it must also be — or partner with — the trust anchor that validates, authorises, and logs those connections. Both t54 Labs and Oasis Security are strong candidates for deeper engagement.

An important macro signal in this gap is the competition between Google's **AP2** (open agent payments protocol) and Coinbase's **x402** crypto-native alternative. If either becomes the de facto standard for agent-to-merchant transactions, the payment identity layer may be commoditised. The unaddressed layer — compliance governance over agent payment authorisation, transaction limits, counterparty eligibility, and audit logging — remains the differentiated infrastructure opportunity.

## Emerging Themes

**Agent identity as regulated infrastructure** is the dominant cluster, with four companies approaching the same problem from different angles (zero-trust, NHI management, identity standards, payment rails). The variety within this cluster suggests the category is still fragmented and an aggregating infrastructure layer has yet to emerge.

**Compliance without disruption** is a notable sub-theme within the compliance gap. The market is validating two distinct go-to-market approaches: high-scale replacement (Bretton AI) and embedded, non-disruptive agents (Sphinx, Uptiq). Both are succeeding, but the non-disruptive approach may have faster enterprise sales cycles in highly regulated institutions where system replacement is politically difficult.

**Incumbent co-investment in agentic infrastructure** is a new pattern this week. Accenture leading Lyzr's round, J.P. Morgan and Wells Fargo co-investing in Rogo, Vanguard and SEI backing Avantos, and BBVA Spark funding TaxDown all signal that financial incumbents are actively selecting infrastructure partners rather than waiting. The pace of this selection process warrants monitoring.

## Research Highlights

Three academic papers published this week provide relevant theoretical grounding. A comprehensive survey of agentic AI across financial services (Aldridge et al., SSRN:5803628) provides the academic map of the transition from passive to fully autonomous financial agents — useful as a framing document for investment memos. A paper on multi-agent modeling crews for financial model risk management (arXiv:2502.05439) demonstrates that agent orchestration for complex regulated financial workflows is technically validated, reinforcing the orchestration and guardrails gaps simultaneously. A study on autonomous, explainable credit risk agents (arXiv:2601.00818) validates that fully autonomous financial decision-making with audit-grade explainability is now feasible — which directly maps to the compliance and personal agent deployment cases the thesis anticipates.

## Looking Ahead

The most actionable signals from this week point in a consistent direction: the regulated infrastructure window is open, incumbents are selecting partners now, and the regulatory environment is hardening the compliance requirement for agent governance. The companies most likely to move forward toward engagement are Overmind, t54 Labs, and Oasis Security — all in the two gaps with the strongest early preference signal. Sphinx in the compliance gap warrants a closer look for its architectural differentiation.

The most important open search is for a portfolio-integrated tax intelligence agent: no current pipeline company meets that specification, and it remains a clear gap within the tax optimisation category.
