# Agentic AI in Financial Services — Weekly Intelligence Report
**Date:** 29 March 2026
**Coverage:** Week ending 29 March 2026 (third report; prior: 20 March 2026)

---

## Overview

Ten new startups entered the pipeline this week — the largest single-week intake since scouting began — bringing the total tracked pipeline to 40 companies across six gap categories. The week delivered 26 market signals across three briefings, with RSA Conference 2026 as the defining event: agentic AI security was the conference's dominant theme, with senior enterprise leaders reporting that securing autonomous agents is now their most urgent infrastructure priority.

The core thesis continues to be validated from both directions. Institutions — Mastercard, JPMorgan, Manulife, Visa — are publicly committing to agentic commerce infrastructure and citing governance and auditability as the primary differentiators. At the startup level, the most significant new entrants are concentrated in exactly the two gaps where our thesis is strongest: Agent Authentication & Security and AI Safety & Guardrails. The market has moved from research into deployment: no new academic papers were published this week, and the practitioner signals — from NIST, the US Treasury, BCG, and the ABA — point to governance architecture as the central operational challenge for financial institutions deploying agentic AI at scale.

## Gap Coverage

### Personal Agent

The single new entry this week — Obin AI — is the strongest Personal Agent candidate to date. Founded by JPMorgan's former Head of AI (Apoorv Saxena) and a Google AI veteran, Obin AI has deployed a production-ready agentic workforce inside financial institutions' existing controls and audit boundaries, already processing over 50,000 annual loan notices for a $500 billion private credit fund at 70–90% time savings. Backed by Motive Partners with a $7 million seed round, the company is engaged with institutions representing over $1 trillion in AUM.

Obin AI's institution-native model is architecturally significant: unlike consumer-facing finance agents, Obin deploys within the institution's own regulatory perimeter. This framing — agents operating inside rather than alongside the governance framework — may represent the more durable model in a world where Mastercard has announced its Virtual C-Suite: AI agents for SME financial services built on 175 billion annual transaction data points and distributed through financial institutions and accounting platforms. The implication is that the "front door" personal finance agent layer may increasingly be claimed by incumbents with proprietary data moats. The regulated infrastructure beneath those agents — not the agent application itself — remains the more defensible opportunity.

The Personal Agent gap now has three pipeline companies (Mine/MoneyGPT, Tendi, Obin AI) and remains the thinnest by company count. Active scouting for institution-focused agentic workflow tools should continue.

### Compliance

Two new companies entered the Compliance gap this week at opposite ends of the scale spectrum.

Harvey raised $200 million at an $11 billion valuation from Singapore's GIC and Sequoia, reaching $190 million in ARR with 1,300 organisations and 100,000+ lawyers on the platform. Harvey's AI agents automate contract analysis, compliance due diligence, and regulatory documentation — with significant penetration into financial services via ISDA documentation, AML policy review, and M&A diligence. GIC's lead investment represents the most substantive sovereign validation of AI compliance infrastructure seen this year. Harvey's current stage places it outside a typical early-stage partnership window, but its scale and trajectory make it a meaningful strategic benchmark.

Zoven (formerly Zivy, Blume Ventures-backed) is an early-signal entry from India: a founding team pivoting from B2B SaaS into AI-native compliance for fintech merchant onboarding — targeting KYC and AML for AI-automated onboarding flows at growing Indian fintechs. It confirms that the compliance-as-a-service category is forming globally, with AI-accelerated onboarding creating compliance gaps that purpose-built platforms are moving to fill.

Within the existing compliance pipeline, Steward ($100 billion in investor AUM on-platform, Motive Partners, same-day onboarding in 80% of cases) remains the highest-priority unvoted candidate in this gap. The ABA and Bank Policy Institute's submission to NIST this week — calling for voluntary governance standards for bank-deployed agents covering approval gates, blast-radius limits, and rollback capabilities — adds further commercial urgency: institutions are defining what governed agentic AI looks like now, and the compliance infrastructure that aligns with those emerging standards earliest will have a structural advantage.

### Tax Optimisation

No new companies entered this week. April — the embedded AI tax platform for wealth management firms, backed by QED Investors with $78 million total raised — remains the gap's most thesis-aligned entry. Its approach to real-time tax simulations embedded in advisor workflows directly addresses the preference for portfolio-integrated tax intelligence rather than annual filing support. The gap has been flat for two consecutive weeks, suggesting search terms are correctly filtering out filing-focused products while the narrower market of portfolio-integrated tools develops more slowly.

### Agent Orchestration

OpenCFO, built by engineers from CrowdStrike, Confluent, and Bloomberg, entered the pipeline with a $2 million seed round to build an AI-native financial operating system for mid-market CFOs — connecting accounts payable, receivable, and treasury to banking infrastructure and ERPs with human-in-loop approvals and audit trails built into the architecture. Its explicit "financial OS" positioning and regulated architecture are directly aligned with the thesis; the limitation is its very early stage.

Eragon — backed at a $100 million post-money valuation on $12 million raised — is building an LLM-native enterprise operating system that replaces ERP, CRM, and business intelligence interfaces with a natural-language prompt layer. It serves as a useful signal that enterprise software replacement via agentic interfaces is a well-capitalised bet, but it lacks financial services specificity for direct thesis alignment.

The more significant developments in this gap were external. JPMorgan Payments announced a global partnership with Mirakl for agentic enterprise commerce, explicitly citing verified agent identity and governance as the competitive differentiators. Manulife selected Akka as infrastructure partner for an agentic AI platform targeting more than $1 billion in enterprise value by 2027, with safety, governance, and explainability as stated design requirements. These are the largest institutions publicly selecting orchestration infrastructure partners — a signal that the enterprise selection window is underway.

### AI Safety & Guardrails

RSAC 2026 elevated this gap to the top of the enterprise security agenda. Two new pipeline entries emerged: Armadin, founded by Kevin Mandia (founder of Mandiant, acquired by Google for $5.4 billion) and backed by Accel, GV, Kleiner Perkins, and the CIA's In-Q-Tel, with $190 million raised at combined seed and Series A — a record early-stage raise for a security company. Armadin deploys autonomous AI security agents to continuously red-team and respond to threats without human intervention. Surf AI raised $57 million from Accel and Cyberstarts to build an agentic security operations platform that ingests identity, cloud, HR, and IT signals, prioritises risk by business impact, and orchestrates remediation with full human oversight and auditability.

Both companies validate the market substantially — Surf AI's auditability and human approval architecture maps closely to what regulated financial environments require. The conference data point that 72% of organisations lack confidence in their AI security strategy represents a large, urgent, and uncaptured demand for governed agent infrastructure.

The combined investment in this gap over two weeks now exceeds $500 million. Overmind — the London-based regulated-industry agent supervision company — remains the standout candidate in this gap, with the highest pipeline score and the strongest confirmed rating.

### Agent Authentication & Security

The fastest-growing gap this week, with three significant new entrants.

Crossmint — backed by Ribbit Capital and Franklin Templeton — provides the full agentic payments stack: agent wallets, virtual Visa and Mastercard cards, stablecoin infrastructure, and KYA (Know Your Agent) credentials that create auditable chains from every agent action back to the authorising human. It is the most complete end-to-end agent payment compliance platform in the pipeline, and Ribbit's specialist fintech focus and Franklin Templeton's asset management position provide direct institutional validation.

imper.ai, founded by Israeli intelligence Unit 8200 veterans and backed by Redpoint and Battery Ventures, launched at RSAC 2026 with a workforce identity security platform that detects AI-driven impersonation and social engineering attacks in real time — confirming that the human behind an enterprise session is genuine. Already deployed in financial services. As AI agents gain delegated access to financial systems, the human-to-agent identity boundary becomes the primary attack surface; imper.ai addresses this directly with production deployment evidence.

InFlow is an earlier-stage agent onboarding and policy compliance platform — enabling AI agents to autonomously transact within pre-approved organisational credentials and spending guardrails, addressing the underserved problem of agent credentialing at the moment of action rather than only at setup.

The gap's existing 5-star candidates — t54 Labs (agent identity standards) and Oasis Security (non-human identity management) — remain the strongest confirmed thesis matches. With this week's additions, the gap now presents a coherent infrastructure stack from identity standards (t54) through NHI management (Oasis) to payment rails and KYA credentials (Crossmint, InFlow) and human-agent boundary security (imper.ai, Alter).

## Emerging Themes

**Agent Payment Infrastructure is crystallising around KYA.** The entry of Crossmint and InFlow, alongside Mastercard's Verifiable Intent open standard (with Google, Fiserv, and IBM already committed), Visa's Agentic Ready programme with 21 European bank partners, and the emergence of "KYA" (Know Your Agent) as a credentialing framework, signals that the agent payment compliance layer is forming rapidly. KYA positions agent credentialing as the regulatory equivalent of KYC for humans — and whoever establishes the standard earliest occupies the same structural position that SWIFT holds for interbank messaging. The compliance layer atop any agentic payment standard is the most defensible infrastructure play.

**RSAC 2026 confirmed agentic security as the enterprise priority.** The conference produced new entrants in autonomous red-teaming, agentic SecOps, workforce identity verification, and hardware-backed human approval gates (IBM WatsonX/Auth0/Yubico). The statistic that 87% of enterprises report increased AI vulnerability risk — combined with the $247 million raised by Armadin and Surf AI in a single week — signals that enterprises are investing in governed agent infrastructure before regulation mandates it. The pattern is consistent with the adoption curve of enterprise cloud security controls a decade ago.

**Incumbents are claiming the front door.** Mastercard's Virtual C-Suite, JPMorgan's Mirakl partnership, and Manulife's $1 billion enterprise value commitment collectively signal that the largest institutions are selecting the orchestration and application layers of agentic finance now. Their framing — governance, verified identity, and auditability as differentiators — validates the regulated infrastructure thesis while simultaneously closing the startup opportunity in the agent application layer. The white space is the compliance, audit, and identity infrastructure those incumbent applications cannot build themselves.

**The ABA/BPI NIST submission is the most important regulatory signal of the week.** The banking industry is proactively defining voluntary governance standards for bank-deployed agents — covering permission structures, approval gates, blast-radius limits, and rollback capabilities — before regulators impose prescriptive rules. Infrastructure certified against those emerging standards will hold a structural advantage when they solidify. The window to align with and potentially shape those standards is open now.

## Competitive Landscape

The pipeline's highest-scoring and most thesis-confirmed candidates remain Overmind (AI Safety & Guardrails, regulated-industry agent supervision, London), t54 Labs (Agent Authentication, agent identity standards), and Oasis Security (Agent Authentication, non-human identity management) — the two 5-star confirmed candidates. No companies have yet moved from initial detection into formal engagement or shortlisting.

The strongest new entry this week across all gaps is Crossmint: Ribbit Capital and Franklin Templeton backing, a complete agent payments stack built directly on Visa and Mastercard rails, and a KYA credentialing architecture aligned with the regulatory vocabulary now forming around agent governance. Harvey at $11 billion — while outside typical partnership parameters — marks an important strategic datapoint: compliance AI for financial services, at scale, now attracts sovereign-wealth-fund capital and enterprise trust at a level comparable to core financial infrastructure companies.

Obin AI (Personal Agent, Motive Partners) is the most compelling new entry on the application side — production deployment at $500 billion-plus institutions and a founding team with direct JPMorgan and Google AI leadership credentials. Its institution-native architecture makes it a closer fit with the regulated infrastructure thesis than the gap label implies.

## Market Intelligence

BCG has sized the agentic AI opportunity for technology service providers at $200 billion, with retail banking as a primary vertical. Separately, industry data puts 76% of financial institutions planning agentic AI adoption, with 82% planning to increase AI investment by more than 25% within two to three years. AI agents are estimated to be generating $262 billion in sales-related revenue across banking currently. The market is no longer hypothetical — the infrastructure and governance layer that enables those agent deployments is revenue-critical.

The US Treasury's AITO and FSOC launched a public-private AI Innovation Series on March 20, bringing together financial institutions, technology firms, and regulators across four roundtables focused on fraud detection, cybersecurity, credit underwriting, and operational risk. This is the most substantive federal initiative on AI in financial services governance to date and signals active co-design of the regulatory landscape — an environment in which early-mover infrastructure relationships carry strategic value beyond the immediate commercial opportunity.
